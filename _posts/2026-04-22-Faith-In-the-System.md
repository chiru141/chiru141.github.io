I was going to the beach this week, the government has completed the phase-1 of the sea-face development Project in Surat. Gujarat. The project (inaugurated march 2026) features a transformed coastline, offering a modern urban beach, promenade, and eco-tourism amenities similar to Mumbai’s Marine Drive **[1]**. 

While going towards the beach, I noticed they were widening the narrow lanes in that area, like always, I felt my heart sink... I don't want the Goa like vibes of this part of city to vanish. that's exactly what makes it beautiful, the "away from city", coastal area feel. Now, it's all being commercialized with various cafes, and modern walkways. As I reached the sea-facing promenade, I saw how beautiful it was. It felt refreshing. So, even though the feelings of the place had to change a bit, it was going to be replaced by the excitement of something more approachable. A little *(I don't know what we could be termed little or more)* discomfort about loosing something, to get to somewhere which might have the potential to be better - would be like a cost we pay for growth? *(I don't know what we humans call better or growth, I think its different for different people, but I think the idea is change is constant and we can't runaway from it, we are constantly loosing and gaining something.)*

Anyway, with this new thought in my head, that if we are prepared to loose something, we might be gaining something - otherwise the transaction won't make sense and we wouldn't want to loose anything at least not without making a fuss.
I thought about the Google hyperscalers and People of Vizag Debate. Government of Andhra is not unintelligent. If they are subsidizing the exact things ( Water, Land, and electricity ) they should be charging for, they must be definitely looking to gain immensely from the transaction. Water, land and electricity are the top 3 considerations for Data centre siting. These are the factors why data centre developers are competing for. Shouldn't we be charging a premium? Why give them a ₹22,002 crore incentive package **[2]** to take what they already need? 

Building a data centre is not growth until it pays, is it? We could be monetizing our resources, but the government has already subsidized them for next 20 years. then, what are we getting? What does a state get? These are some of the questions I had. 

I know the answer without thinking is - Growth.

but, in what terms? Job creation? Reports on the Vizag AI Hub investment (est. ₹87,000 crore) suggest it will generate roughly only 200–500 direct technical jobs **[3]**. To put that in perspective, a traditional factory with that investment would employ 20,000+. 

So then, what does growth mean here: 

After quite a bit of back and forth with Gemini, youtube videos and latest articles, I think it comes down to 3 things: 

1. **Googles funding the Grid Upgradation for Renewable Energy:** Google’s 1GW requirement forces a massive upgrade of the local power substations. The government gets a world-class power grid for the whole district, funded largely by the requirements of one company.  

Will Google be motivated to do this though? Probably, if it adheres to it's target of being 24*7 carbon neutral energy consumer. 

Does it need to? is it mandated? 

As of April 13, 2026, the Andhra Pradesh government granted Google’s subsidiary, Raiden Infotech, a "Deemed Distribution License" (DISCOM status) **[4]**. Google is now legally its own power utility. To maintain this status, the government has mandated that at least 51% of their energy must come from renewable sources.

2. **Reversing the brain drain challenge:** As Google builds this hyperscaler, it becomes the foundation of an ecosystem. Other companies that work with data centres - like ISPs (Internet Service Providers) and start-ups come to the city too bringing down digitalization prices for businesses and residents alike. it will reduce the cost of digital for local businesses (will it? - apparently the latency drop reduces prices for AI and finTech firms that require real-time processing) **[5]** and essentially reverse the "brain drain". If a startup in Vizag has the same internet speed and infrastructure as a startup in Bangalore or Silicon valley, our engineers don't have to leave their homes to find a better life. 

3. **Building Geopolitical Leverage:** Nations might store their sovereign data in Vizag. So? This turns Vizag into a "Digital Safe Haven," giving India diplomatic and economic leverage to negotiate better trade deals, more secure energy partnerships, and a seat at the head of the global AI table **[6]**.

About the water issue - Estimates suggest the hub will consume 68 million liters a day—roughly 12% of the city’s supply **[7]**. 

Union Ministers recently estimated that this 1GW hub will generate roughly ₹10,518 crore ($1.25 billion) annually for the state’s GDP by 2028-2032 **[8]**. The idea is that the tax revenue—which could easily exceed ₹500 crore annually (even at a modest 10%)—is used immediately to build the ₹1,000 crore desalination plant resolving the water stress in the region. 

So, If the government builds the desalination plant with the data center's taxes, the community lowers its risk of drinking water stress and enjoys the benefits of being a global tech hub. If they don't, they just get a fancy tech park and dry taps. So, the idea is not itself bad, but without proper guardrails and execution - it might fail.

I think it's time we have a little bit more faith in our governments, not blind faith, but a 'working faith' that holds our governments to the promises they make in exchange for our resources while actively working with them towards defining progress.
